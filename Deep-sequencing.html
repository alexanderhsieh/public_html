Overview:

Next-generation sequencing (NGS) has become ubiquitous in biomedical research with numerous applications. This course will provide an in-depth introduction to principles of modern sequencing, key computational algorithms and statistical models, and applications in disease genetics, cancer and fundamental biology. It will cover genome, exome and transcriptome sequencing approaches.  Emphasis will be placed on understanding the interplay between experimental design, data acquisition, and data analysis so that students can apply these powerful tools in their own research.

Topics:

  • History and development of modern sequencing technologies
  • Introduction to statistics and algorithms
  • Genome sequencing and genetics of Mendelian and complex diseases (analysis and laboratory techniques for whole genome and exome sequencing)
  • RNA-Seq and transcriptional regulation (analysis and laboratory techniques for expression profiling, CLIP-Seq, ribosome profiling, micro-RNA profiling).
  • Cancer genomics
  • Emerging third and fourth generation sequencing technologies and single cell analysis.

Goals:

  • To acquire introductory knowledge of modern high-throughput sequencing approaches including instrumentation and laboratory techniques.
  • To understand computational methods for analyzing genome sequencing data.
  • To formulate a biological question and investigate it by analyzing existing sequencing data.

Requirements:

This course is intended for graduate students or senior undergraduates interested in learning state-of-the-art sequencing approaches and their applications in biological and medical research.

Required: Basic knowledge in molecular biology, probability, and statistics

Preferred: Working knowledge of UNIX and a programming language (R, MATLAB, Python, or Perl). Basic knowledge of genetics would also be helpful.

Grading:

  • Class Participation: 10%
  • Journal Club Presentation: 20%
  • Midterm Exam: 20%
  • Final Project: 50%

The course will be highly interactive and require class participation on which each student will be graded (10% of course grade). Throughout the course, there will be ten journal club presentations on pre-assigned papers. Teams of 2-3 students will be randomly assigned to a week/paper during Week 1 for 30-40 minute journal club presentations (20% of course grade). There will be a take-home midterm exam in Week 8 (20% of course grade). Students will assemble into teams with 3-4 members to pursue a Final Project as a group (50% of course grade). Abstracts will be presented in Week 8. Each team will produce a paper describing their results and deliver a final oral presentation in the last two weeks of the course.
